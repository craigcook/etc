Design Principles

just the basics

Design is a complicated thing, and hard to teach. Most designers spend years studying it, but I'm going to try to tell you as much as I can in the time we have. The point is to get you thinking about design and looking for these principles.

Unity

When all elements are in agreement and no one part is more important than the whole.

Unity is one of the primary goals of design. Unfortunately it's hard to explain exactly how to achieve it. There are a lot of factors that contribute to a unified design, and numerous methods to get there.

Balance

When elements are distributed in a pleasing arrangement, achieving a state of visual equilibrium.

We have a natural sense of balance. You have juices in your head that slosh around and tell you when you're falling. Balance is necessary for survival and we can recognize it without even thinking about it. Balance creates a sense of harmony, where everything just "feels right."

Symmetry

Also known as "formal balance," each side is a mirror image of the other.

Symmetry

The sides don't have to be identical mirror images, they may carry the same "weight" even though they differ in size, shape, color, etc.

Symmetry

Both sides take up about the same space and have the same level of visual complexity.

Symmetry

Even if the sides are very different there's still a sense of equal weight and symmetrical balance.

Asymmetry

Also known as "informal balance." This is a sort of imbalanced balance, where the two sides of the design are completely different but still balance each other through other means, such as mass vs proximity.

Asymmetry

Mass vs repetition

Asymmetry

Mass vs complexity

Asymmetry

You can also deliberately upset balance to create tension. When you know the rules you can carefully break them.

Not just balance

Balance is only one important part of a unified design. Some other principles, in rapid fire...

Repetition

Establish consistency and repeat that throughout. Repeat it enough times and it becomes a pattern.

Patterns

Patterns and repetition establish expectations.

Similarity

Vary the pattern and change expectations. When similar objects are clustered together our eye naturally sorts them into groups.

Proximity

Space between elements (or space between groups) can break up patterns and separate groups. Suddenly we have two distinct groups of otherwise identical objects simply by adding a little space.

Alignment

Our eyes seem to seek out lines, and when we're mentally grouping similar things we want to line them up. When elements don't align it forms an imbalance and breaks unity (sometimes this is good, usually it's bad).

Alignment

Aligning elements is more naturally pleasing, even when alignment isn't perfect.

Dominance

Create focal points and draw attention to key elements without sacrificing the unity of the whole.

When you design for the web, an almost all cases, you're relaying information. And in almost all cases, some information is more important than other information on the same page. The main article is more important than the sidebar, the sidebar is more important than the legalese, and so on.

Contrast

size and proportion

To make something stand out and have greater dominance, it must contrast with other elements that may otherwise be similar. The dominant thing might be bigger and take up more space.

Contrast

color

Or it might be a different color.

Contrast

complexity

Or has more visual complexity. The eye will be drawn to and linger over things that are harder to figure out.

Contrast

a little of everything

Or it might be a combination of aspects. Color, shape, complexity, breaking the expected pattern, etc. When the eye expects something it is drawn to things that don't meet that expectation.
